# Hick's Law

Hick's Law states that the time it takes to make a decision increases logarithmically with the number of options presented. More choices mean longer deliberation, higher cognitive load, and greater likelihood of decision paralysis or abandonment. Simplifying the choice set speeds up decisions and increases conversion.

Apply this when designing menus, navigation, onboarding flows, pricing pages, or any interface where the user must choose. It is most critical at high-stakes decision points like signup, checkout, or plan selection.

**Steps to apply:**
1. Count the number of choices presented at each decision point.
2. Reduce options to the minimum viable set — can you remove, hide, or group any?
3. Use progressive disclosure to reveal secondary options only after the primary choice is made.
4. Provide smart defaults or recommendations to reduce the cognitive burden.

**Common mistakes:** Offering every option upfront to seem "flexible." Confusing fewer choices with less functionality — you can still have depth, just revealed progressively. Ignoring that even visual clutter (decorative elements, competing CTAs) adds to perceived choice complexity.
