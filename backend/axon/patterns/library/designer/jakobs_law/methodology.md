# Jakob's Law

Jakob's Law states that users spend most of their time on other sites and apps. They build mental models from those cumulative experiences and expect yours to work the same way. When you deviate from established conventions, you force users to learn instead of do. Innovation should happen in your value proposition, not in how your navigation works.

Apply this law when making layout, interaction, or component decisions. Before inventing a new pattern, check what users already expect from similar products. It is especially critical for utilitarian interfaces like dashboards, forms, and e-commerce flows.

**Steps to apply:**
1. Identify the 3-5 products your users spend the most time in.
2. Study those products' conventions for the interaction you are designing (navigation, search, settings, etc.).
3. Default to the established pattern unless you have strong, evidence-based reasons to deviate.
4. If you must break convention, provide clear affordances and guidance to bridge the gap.

**Common mistakes:** Equating convention-following with laziness or lack of creativity. Innovating on standard interactions that users need to be automatic. Testing novel patterns only with team members who already understand the product.
