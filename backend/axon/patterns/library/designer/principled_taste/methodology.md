# Principled Taste

Principled taste is the discipline of never stopping at "it feels wrong." Gut reactions are valuable signals but poor arguments. When something feels off, the designer's job is to trace that feeling back to a specific, articulable design principle that is being violated. This transforms subjective taste into objective critique that teams can discuss, debate, and act on.

Apply this during design reviews, feedback sessions, or any time you or a stakeholder has an intuitive negative reaction to a design. It is also a useful self-check before presenting work.

**Steps to apply:**
1. Notice the gut reaction — something feels off, ugly, confusing, or wrong.
2. Pause and ask: "What principle is being violated here?"
3. Name the principle explicitly — consistency, hierarchy, proximity, alignment, contrast, feedback, etc.
4. Reframe the critique around the principle: "This feels off because the visual hierarchy does not match the task priority."
5. Propose a fix that addresses the principle, not just the symptom.

**Common mistakes:** Accepting "I just don't like it" as valid design feedback. Overriding gut feelings entirely in favor of data — intuition trained by experience is a real signal. Citing principles to justify personal preference rather than genuine design issues.
