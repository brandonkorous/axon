# Theory of Constraints

The Theory of Constraints states that every system has exactly one constraint that limits its overall throughput. Like a chain that breaks at its weakest link, improving anything other than the bottleneck is wasted effort. The system can only move as fast as its slowest part allows.

Apply this pattern whenever a process feels slow or output is plateauing despite effort. It is especially useful when teams are busy but results are not improving — a classic sign that work is piling up before an unidentified constraint.

**Steps to apply:**
1. Identify the constraint — look for where work queues up or waits.
2. Exploit the constraint — squeeze maximum output from it without spending money.
3. Subordinate everything else — adjust all other processes to serve the constraint.
4. Elevate the constraint — invest to increase its capacity.
5. Repeat — once resolved, a new constraint will emerge. Go back to step 1.

**Common mistakes:** Trying to optimize every step simultaneously instead of focusing on the one bottleneck. Confusing busy teams with constrained teams. Fixing the wrong constraint because you measured activity instead of throughput.
