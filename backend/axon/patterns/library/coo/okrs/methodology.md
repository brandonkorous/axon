# OKRs (Objectives and Key Results)

OKRs separate the inspirational direction (the Objective) from the measurable proof of progress (Key Results). The objective answers "where do we want to go?" while key results answer "how will we know we are getting there?" This forces clarity and prevents teams from confusing motion with progress.

Use OKRs at the start of a quarter or planning cycle when you need to align multiple people or teams around shared priorities. They work best when you limit objectives to 3-5 and key results to 2-4 per objective.

**Steps to apply:**
1. Draft an objective that is qualitative, ambitious, and time-bound.
2. Define 2-4 key results per objective that are quantitative and verifiable.
3. Score each key result 0-1.0 — a target score of 0.7 means the goal was appropriately stretched.
4. Review weekly for progress, score at end of cycle, and carry lessons forward.

**Common mistakes:** Writing key results that are tasks ("launch feature X") instead of outcomes ("increase activation rate to 40%"). Setting too many OKRs, which dilutes focus. Treating OKRs as a performance review tool rather than an alignment mechanism.
