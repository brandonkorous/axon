# One-Way & Two-Way Doors

This mental model separates decisions into two categories based on reversibility. A one-way door is a decision that is nearly impossible or extremely costly to undo — shutting down a product line, signing a 10-year lease, or making a public commitment. A two-way door is a decision you can walk back through cheaply — changing a price, testing a feature, or adjusting a workflow.

Apply this pattern whenever a decision feels stuck in committee or is consuming disproportionate deliberation time. Ask: "If we get this wrong, can we reverse it within weeks at low cost?" If yes, it is a two-way door — move fast, delegate downward, and iterate. If no, it is a one-way door — slow down, gather data, involve senior judgment.

**Steps:** (1) State the decision clearly. (2) List what happens if you need to reverse it — cost, time, reputation damage. (3) Classify it as one-way or two-way. (4) Match your process intensity to the classification.

The most common mistake is treating two-way doors like one-way doors, which kills speed. The second mistake is assuming something is reversible when customers or partners have already built dependencies on your choice.
