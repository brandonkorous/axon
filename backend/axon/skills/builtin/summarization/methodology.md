# Summarization Methodology

## 1. Identify the Purpose

Before summarizing, determine WHY this summary is needed:

- **Inform**: The reader wants to understand what happened or what something is.
- **Decide**: The reader needs enough information to make a choice.
- **Act**: The reader needs to know what to do next.
- **Share**: The reader will forward this to others who were not present.

The purpose dictates what to include and what to cut. A summary for decision-making emphasizes trade-offs and options. A summary for action emphasizes tasks and owners.

## 2. Extract Key Points

Read the source material and pull out the essential information:

- **What is the core message?** If the source had to be reduced to one sentence, what survives?
- **What are the supporting points?** Identify 3-7 facts or arguments that support the core message.
- **What is new or surprising?** Highlight anything that the reader would not already know or expect.
- **What is actionable?** Flag decisions made, tasks assigned, or next steps identified.

Discard filler, repetition, tangents, and pleasantries. Ruthless cutting is a feature, not a bug.

## 3. Separate Facts From Opinions

Clearly distinguish between:

- **Facts**: Verifiable statements, data points, measurements, events that occurred.
- **Opinions**: Interpretations, predictions, recommendations, value judgments.
- **Consensus**: Points where all parties agree.
- **Disputes**: Points where parties disagree — note who holds which position.

When summarizing discussions or meetings, attribute opinions to their source. Do not present one person's opinion as the group's conclusion unless it was explicitly agreed upon.

## 4. Organize by Importance, Not Chronology

Summaries are not transcripts. Do NOT replay events in the order they happened.

Instead, use the inverted pyramid:

1. Most important conclusion or outcome first.
2. Key supporting details second.
3. Background and context third.
4. Minor details last (or omit entirely).

This ensures that a reader who stops at any point has already absorbed the most valuable information.

## 5. Produce Three Layers

Always deliver the summary in three layers of increasing detail:

### Layer 1: One-Liner
A single sentence that captures the essential takeaway. This is the subject line of the email, the headline, the TLDR. Maximum 25 words.

### Layer 2: Executive Summary
3-5 bullet points covering the key findings, decisions, or conclusions. Each bullet should be a complete thought — no fragments or vague references. Target: 50-100 words total.

### Layer 3: Detailed Summary
A structured narrative (3-8 paragraphs) covering:
- Context and background
- Key points with supporting evidence
- Decisions made and their rationale
- Open questions or unresolved issues
- Next steps and action items

## 6. End With Action Items

If the source material contains any action items, decisions, or next steps, extract them into a dedicated section at the end:

- **What** needs to happen
- **Who** is responsible (if identified)
- **When** it is due (if specified)

If no action items exist, explicitly state: "No action items identified." This prevents the reader from wondering if they missed something.

## 7. Quality Checks

Before delivering a summary, verify:

- [ ] Does the one-liner accurately represent the whole? (No clickbait or misleading framing.)
- [ ] Could someone who reads only the executive summary make correct decisions?
- [ ] Are facts and opinions clearly distinguished?
- [ ] Are all action items captured with owners and deadlines where available?
- [ ] Is the summary shorter than the source? (If not, you are not summarizing.)
