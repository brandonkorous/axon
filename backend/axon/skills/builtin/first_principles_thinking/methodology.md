# First Principles Thinking Methodology

You are performing a structured first-principles analysis using the Three Layers of Knowledge framework. This framework separates how you know things into distinct layers, then checks whether conventional wisdom actually holds for THIS specific case.

## Layer 1: Tried and True

**Question:** "What does conventional wisdom say?"

Identify the standard patterns, established approaches, and industry norms for this problem:

- What would a textbook or experienced practitioner recommend?
- What are the "obvious" answers that most people would give?
- What patterns have been proven over time?
- What assumptions are baked into these approaches?

List each pattern explicitly. Name the sources or traditions they come from. For each one, note the **underlying assumption** — the thing that must be true for this pattern to work.

**Risk of Layer 1:** Assuming the obvious is right. Conventional wisdom persists through inertia, not just correctness. The world changes faster than textbooks update.

## Layer 2: New and Popular

**Question:** "What are the current best practices?"

Identify what's trending, what thought leaders recommend, and what the cutting-edge approach looks like:

- What would a well-read practitioner recommend today?
- What new tools, frameworks, or approaches have gained traction?
- Where does Layer 2 update or contradict Layer 1?
- What's the evidence for the new approaches — battle-tested or hype-driven?

For each practice, note whether it's backed by **real-world results** or primarily by **enthusiasm and marketing**. Distinguish signal from noise.

**Risk of Layer 2:** Crowd mania. Popularity is not proof. "Everyone is doing it" is the weakest form of evidence.

## Layer 3: First Principles

**Question:** "If I had no prior knowledge and could only reason from the fundamental constraints and goals, what would I do?"

This is the critical layer. Forget everything from Layers 1 and 2. Start from scratch:

1. **Define the actual goal.** Not the conventional framing of the goal — the raw, underlying objective. What are we actually trying to achieve?
2. **List the hard constraints.** Physics, economics, human psychology, regulatory requirements. Things that cannot be wished away.
3. **Remove soft constraints.** "We've always done it this way," "the industry standard is," "best practice says" — these are not constraints, they are habits.
4. **Build up from fundamentals.** Given only the goal and hard constraints, what is the simplest path from here to there?
5. **Check your work.** Does your first-principles solution look like Layers 1 or 2? If so, maybe the conventional approach is genuinely optimal. If not, you may have found something.

## Eureka Check

Compare your Layer 3 conclusion against Layers 1 and 2:

- **Layer 3 agrees with Layer 1 and 2:** The conventional approach is probably correct for this case. No eureka — but you now hold the position with conviction rather than habit.
- **Layer 3 agrees with Layer 2 but not Layer 1:** The industry is updating for good reason. Not a eureka, but worth noting the shift.
- **Layer 3 contradicts both Layer 1 AND Layer 2:** This is a **EUREKA MOMENT**. Your first-principles reasoning suggests that the conventional AND the trendy approaches are both wrong for THIS specific case.

For each Eureka Moment, explain:
1. **What the conventional approach assumes** (and why that assumption is wrong here)
2. **What first principles reveal** (the insight)
3. **Why this case is different** from the general case where conventional wisdom works
4. **The risk of being wrong** — first principles can be wrong too. What would you need to see to abandon this insight?

## Synthesis

Produce a final recommendation that:

1. Acknowledges what's proven (Layer 1 patterns that still apply)
2. Incorporates what's improving (Layer 2 updates with real evidence)
3. Highlights what's new (Layer 3 insights, especially Eureka Moments)
4. States the confidence level — how certain are you that the first-principles approach is better?

## Rules

- Layer 3 is not automatically better than Layers 1 and 2. Sometimes conventional wisdom is correct. The point is to **check**, not to rebel.
- Be honest about which layer each recommendation comes from. "I recommend X" is less useful than "I recommend X (Layer 1 consensus, Layer 3 confirms)."
- Eureka Moments are rare. If you find one on every question, your standard is too low.
- Name your assumptions at every layer. The value of this framework is in making assumptions explicit so they can be challenged.
- If the question is simple or well-understood, say so. Not every problem needs first-principles analysis — sometimes the standard approach is just... right.
