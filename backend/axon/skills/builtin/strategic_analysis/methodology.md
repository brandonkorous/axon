# Strategic Analysis Methodology

You are performing structured strategic analysis. Apply multiple frameworks, then synthesize across them to surface patterns that no single framework reveals alone.

## Phase 1: Problem Framing

Before applying any framework, establish clarity:

- **Decision context**: What decision or understanding is this analysis meant to inform? If unclear, ask.
- **Entity under analysis**: Company, product, market, or initiative. Define boundaries precisely.
- **Time horizon**: Are we analyzing current state, near-term (1 year), or long-term (3-5 years)?
- **Stakeholder perspective**: Whose interests are we optimizing for?
- **Constraints**: Budget, regulatory, technical, or organizational limits that bound the solution space.

State these explicitly before proceeding. Ambiguity here invalidates everything downstream.

## Phase 2: Data Gathering

For each framework you will apply, identify the data you need:

- **Internal data**: Capabilities, resources, financials, team, technology, processes.
- **External data**: Market size, growth rates, competitors, customer segments, regulatory landscape, macro trends.
- **Qualitative signals**: Customer sentiment, employee morale, brand perception, ecosystem momentum.

Flag data gaps explicitly. Do not fabricate market data — state assumptions and mark them.

## Phase 3: Framework Application

Apply each framework completely. Do not skip sections because they seem less relevant.

### SWOT Analysis
- **Strengths**: Internal advantages. Be specific — "good team" is useless; "3 senior ML engineers with production recommendation system experience" is useful.
- **Weaknesses**: Internal disadvantages. Be honest. Optimistic SWOT is worthless SWOT.
- **Opportunities**: External favorable conditions the entity could exploit.
- **Threats**: External unfavorable conditions that could cause harm.

Cross-link items: Which strengths can exploit which opportunities? Which weaknesses amplify which threats?

### Porter's Five Forces
Rate each force as **low**, **medium**, or **high** with specific justification:
1. **Threat of new entrants** — barriers to entry, capital requirements, network effects.
2. **Bargaining power of suppliers** — concentration, switching costs, differentiation.
3. **Bargaining power of buyers** — concentration, price sensitivity, alternatives.
4. **Threat of substitutes** — functional alternatives, price-performance tradeoffs.
5. **Competitive rivalry** — number of competitors, market growth, differentiation, exit barriers.

### Competitive Mapping
Position key players on a 2x2 matrix. Choose axes that reveal strategic differentiation (e.g., price vs. breadth, enterprise vs. consumer, platform vs. point solution). List 3-6 competitors with brief profiles.

### Market Sizing
Use top-down and bottom-up approaches. Show your math. Distinguish TAM, SAM, and SOM. State all assumptions explicitly.

## Phase 4: Cross-Framework Synthesis

This is where the real insight lives. After completing individual frameworks:

1. **Pattern identification**: What themes appear in 2+ frameworks? These are high-confidence insights.
2. **Contradictions**: Where do frameworks disagree? Investigate why — this often reveals the most interesting strategic tensions.
3. **Blind spots**: What did no framework address? Consider second-order effects, timing, and execution risk.

## Phase 5: Recommendations

For each recommendation:

- **Action**: Specific, concrete next step (not vague strategic platitudes).
- **Rationale**: Which framework findings support this? Reference specific evidence.
- **Confidence**: High / Medium / Low, based on data quality and framework agreement.
- **Priority**: Must-do / Should-do / Could-do, based on impact and urgency.
- **Risks**: What could go wrong if this recommendation is followed?
- **Dependencies**: What must be true for this to work?

Order recommendations by priority, then confidence.

## Quality Checklist

- [ ] Problem framing is explicit and specific
- [ ] Each framework is applied completely, not selectively
- [ ] Data assumptions are flagged and distinguished from facts
- [ ] Cross-framework patterns are identified
- [ ] Recommendations are actionable (a team could execute on them this quarter)
- [ ] Confidence levels reflect actual evidence quality
- [ ] Optimism bias is checked — weaknesses and threats are given equal weight
